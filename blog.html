Field notes

Weekly log from my NSOC Analyst role at UMass Boston — what I worked on, what I learned, and what actually happened.

Week of June 9, 2026 · NSOC Analyst · Day Shift

First incidents, first threat hunt

Started the NSOC analyst role this week and went straight into Microsoft Sentinel — reviewed and documented three incidents (two informational, one low-severity) and sent my findings up to my supervisor. Also got Tenable access set up alongside the rest of the team, with vulnerability management scoped across roughly 185 devices on the UMass Boston network.

The other big piece this week was kicking off a threat hunting project. My supervisor walked the team through the idea that threat hunting starts with a hypothesis, not a tool — you identify software in use across the organization, research what could realistically go wrong with it, and prioritize based on attack surface and how valuable the target is. That meant getting familiar with CVSS and EPSS scoring and doing actual research in the NVD to back up the hypothesis instead of guessing.

Week of June 16–18, 2026 · NSOC Analyst · Day Shift

Chasing down a smishing report

A colleague on the team flagged an email report about a staff member who'd received a suspicious text message, and the source wasn't obvious at first glance — so this week turned into a crash course in manual phishing and smishing investigation. That meant checking URLs against reputation tools like VirusTotal, digging into message headers, and looking for the usual social engineering red flags: vague notices, links that don't match where they claim to go, missing recipient info, and pressure to act immediately.

The biggest lesson from this week: a real, recognizable company name in an email doesn't mean the email is legitimate, and automated tools won't always give you a clean verdict. Sometimes you have to verify the company and the URL independently before you can call something safe or malicious. I also practiced writing the recommendation itself in plain language — a security finding is only useful if a non-technical person can actually act on it.

Week of June 21–27, 2026 · Vulnerability Management / NSOC Analyst · Day Shift

Learning Tenable end to end

This was the week Tenable stopped being theoretical. I learned the full asset-tagging workflow — creating tags by hostname, finding a machine's hostname or IPv4 address from the command line when the hostname search came up empty, and searching/filtering assets to attach the right tag. From there it was setting up Nessus Agent scans, scheduling them, and adding email notifications so I'd know when a scan actually finished instead of checking manually.

On the findings side, I reviewed real vulnerabilities on endpoints — including a high-severity WinVerifyTrust signature validation issue (CVE-2013-3900, mitigated via the EnableCertPaddingCheck setting) and a medium-severity Microsoft Edge Chromium vulnerability — and proposed remediation steps for both. I also got a first look at ServiceNow and AbuseIP ticket workflows, sat in on how DHS incident reports get filled out, and learned the basics of DMARC, SPF, and DCAM for email authentication and domain spoofing prevention. A senior teammate also covered BitLocker (encrypts the drive, not individual files) and core networking concepts — IPAM, DNS, DHCP, static IPs, and MAC address pairing. We also talked through why legacy systems like old Windows 7 or Windows 98 boxes sometimes have to stay in service, and how air-gapping or DMZ isolation is used as a compensating control when you can't just patch or retire them.

Week of June 29 – July 4, 2026 · Scan Vulnerability Manager / NSOC Analyst · Day Shift

Infoblox, firewalls, and a quota lesson the hard way

A senior teammate introduced DNS ticket workflows in Infoblox this week — assigning IPs to users and cleaning up stale or unused IPs and servers — and I signed up for the Infoblox training platform to go deeper on it. She also walked through how firewall rules stop unwanted incoming traffic, and talked about why every organization needs a WISP (Written Information Security Program), including the frameworks used to secure devices across a whole campus network.

I also learned a practical lesson the hard way: our Tenable subscription hit its scanned-asset limit, which disabled account access the next day. It was a good real-world reminder that managing a vulnerability scanning platform means watching license and asset quotas, not just the vulnerabilities themselves. Separately, my supervisor had the team pull a few desktops from storage to check readiness before deployment — confirming required provisioning software (including Autopilot) was actually present and that the machine showed up correctly in Defender. We caught a couple of machines missing pieces of that setup before they went out.

Week of July 6, 2026 · NSOC Analyst · Day Shift

Going back to fundamentals: identity and access

A lot of the ticket and incident work over the past month kept circling back to identity and access — who's allowed to touch what, and how a system actually confirms that. So this week I set aside time to study the fundamentals properly instead of just pattern-matching from tickets: the CIA triad, AAA, SSO, and the two protocols that make SSO work under the hood, Kerberos and SAML.

The CIA triad — Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability — is the model most security decisions get measured against. Confidentiality is keeping data readable only by people who are supposed to read it; integrity is making sure it isn't altered without detection; availability is making sure the system is actually up when someone legitimately needs it. Nearly every control I work with in the SOC maps back to one of these three, which made it a useful lens for re-reading my own incident notes from earlier weeks.

AAA — Authentication, Authorization, Accounting — is the practical framework built on top of that. Authentication answers "who are you" (password, MFA, certificate). Authorization answers "what are you allowed to do" once you're recognized. Accounting is the audit trail — logging what was actually done, which is exactly the kind of record I rely on when documenting findings in ServiceNow or reconstructing a timeline for an incident.

Single Sign-On (SSO) is where authentication gets centralized: log in once, and that session is trusted across multiple applications instead of re-entering credentials everywhere. That convenience depends on a trust relationship between an identity provider and each application, which is where Kerberos and SAML come in. Kerberos is the older, ticket-based protocol common in on-prem Windows/Active Directory environments — a client authenticates once to a Key Distribution Center and receives a ticket-granting ticket, then uses that to request service tickets for individual resources without re-sending a password each time. SAML is the XML-based standard used for web and cloud SSO — an identity provider authenticates the user and passes a signed assertion to the service provider, which trusts that assertion instead of checking credentials itself. Same underlying goal as Kerberos, different era and different transport.

Tying it together: AAA is the framework, SSO is a convenience layer built on top of authentication, and Kerberos/SAML are two different mechanisms for making that trust relationship actually work depending on whether you're in a legacy on-prem environment or a modern web/cloud one. Useful context for reading account and access-related alerts with more confidence instead of just recognizing the keywords.

Week of July 13–18, 2026 · NSOC Analyst · Day Shift

Air gaps, email flow, and studying for Network+

A senior colleague ran a scenario question at the start of the week: how would you secure a Network Attached Storage device that's under constant attack? I worked through a few increasingly complicated answers before landing near the right one — pull it off the network temporarily. The actual answer was simpler and more absolute: take it off the network entirely. Because there's a separate backup platform covering the NAS, that isolation is an air gap, not a service outage. Good reminder that the correct control is sometimes the blunt one, and that it's only viable because something else already covers availability.

We also reviewed ServiceNow tickets with a technical lead, including a request to bring in a new AI tool. It had been routed to us, but the call belongs to the AI Governance Subcommittee — the open question isn't whether the tool works, it's whether the vendor retains submitted data or uses it for model training. The goal is data flowing in and out without being kept or repurposed. Useful example of a security decision that isn't technical at all: the risk is contractual and the control is a review process.

Midweek was a full walkthrough of inbound email security — the path a message takes before it reaches an inbox. Mail from an external sender hits Microsoft's infrastructure first, where IP reputation and abuse handling can block known-bad senders outright. It then passes the DNS/web security layer, moves through transport rules for routing and filtering, and reaches Defender (ATP) for malware, phishing, and malicious link/attachment scanning. There are enforcement points at each stage where mail gets blocked or allowed through a whitelist. After delivery, Abnormal adds behavioral and anomaly-based detection, and DLP/Purview enforces classification and data protection on the content itself. We closed by reviewing Defender rules that turned out to be redundant — Abnormal was already catching what they were written for. Layered defense is the goal, but overlapping rules across two products is maintenance cost without added coverage.

The rest of the week went to Network+ practice questions — logging why I missed the ones I got wrong rather than just noting the right answer — plus Infoblox lab training on DNS records, DHCP scopes and reservations, and IPAM in the Grid. Studying those together helped: the Infoblox work is the Network+ material with actual consequences attached. Finished by going back through the ServiceNow queue to catch up on updates to open incidents.
