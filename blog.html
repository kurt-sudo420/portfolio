Field notes

Weekly log from my NSOC Analyst role — what I worked on, what I learned, and what actually happened.

Week of June 9, 2026 · NSOC Analyst · Day Shift

First incidents, first threat hunt

Started the NSOC analyst role this week and went straight into Microsoft Sentinel — reviewed and documented a handful of incidents (mostly informational, one low-severity) and sent my findings up to my supervisor. Also got Tenable access set up alongside the rest of the team and started learning how vulnerability management is scoped across an enterprise environment.

The other big piece this week was kicking off a threat hunting project. My supervisor walked the team through the idea that threat hunting starts with a hypothesis, not a tool — you identify software in use across the organization, research what could realistically go wrong with it, and prioritize based on attack surface and how valuable the target is. That meant getting familiar with CVSS and EPSS scoring and doing actual research in the NVD to back up the hypothesis instead of guessing.

Week of June 16–18, 2026 · NSOC Analyst · Day Shift

Chasing down a smishing report

A colleague on the team flagged a report about a suspicious text message, and the source wasn't obvious at first glance — so this week turned into a crash course in manual phishing and smishing investigation. That meant checking URLs against reputation tools like VirusTotal, digging into message headers, and looking for the usual social engineering red flags: vague notices, links that don't match where they claim to go, missing recipient info, and pressure to act immediately.

The biggest lesson from this week: a real, recognizable company name in an email doesn't mean the email is legitimate, and automated tools won't always give you a clean verdict. Sometimes you have to verify the company and the URL independently before you can call something safe or malicious. I also practiced writing the recommendation itself in plain language — a security finding is only useful if a non-technical person can actually act on it.

Week of June 21–27, 2026 · Vulnerability Management / NSOC Analyst · Day Shift

Learning Tenable end to end

This was the week Tenable stopped being theoretical. I learned the full asset-tagging workflow — creating tags by hostname, finding a machine's hostname or IPv4 address from the command line when the hostname search came up empty, and searching/filtering assets to attach the right tag. From there it was setting up Nessus Agent scans, scheduling them, and adding email notifications so I'd know when a scan actually finished
